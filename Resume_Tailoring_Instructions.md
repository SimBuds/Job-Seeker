## 3. Tailoring Workflow

Follow this order. Do not skip steps.

1. **Read the full job description.** Identify three buckets:
   - **Must-haves** (years required, primary stack, hard credentials).
   - **Nice-to-haves / bonus** (the section often labelled "Plus," "Preferred," or "Nice to Have"). These are where Casey's AI tooling and Contentful work often shine.
   - **Domain context** (banking, healthcare, retail, marketing, etc.).

2. **Map each must-have and nice-to-have to Section 2.** For each, mark one of:
   - ✅ Direct match (Casey uses this in production).
   - ◐ Adjacent match (Casey has a transferable equivalent — e.g., Liquid/HubL → AmpScript, Playwright → Cypress, Jest → Mocha).
   - ▢ Coursework only (Casey learned it at GBC but has not used it professionally).
   - ✗ Gap (Casey does not have this).

3. **Decide whether to tailor or decline.** If three or more must-haves are gaps, tell Casey the role is not a realistic fit and recommend skipping it. The two tailored resumes already in the conversation history were borderline — that is the floor.

4. **Rewrite the Summary** (3–5 sentences). It should:
   - Lead with what the job actually wants (e.g., "Full-stack developer with hands-on REST API experience…" not the generic "Full-stack JavaScript developer…").
   - Mention 2–3 must-have keywords from the JD that Casey has, in his actual voice.
   - Include the AI/LLM line whenever the job mentions AI, generative AI, RAG, prompt engineering, ML, or "modern tooling" as a bonus.
   - Mention the GBC diploma + Dean's List once.
   - Never claim years of experience Casey doesn't have.

5. **Re-order and re-categorize the Skills section.**
   - Lead with the category most relevant to the job (e.g., "Email & Web Markup" for an email role; "Languages" for a polyglot role).
   - Move skills inside the categories so the JD's keywords appear first.
   - Drop categories that are irrelevant clutter for the target role (e.g., for a backend role, "CMS & E-Commerce" can be demoted to a single line at the bottom).
   - Keep the "Familiar" honesty signal — promoting a "Familiar" skill to a primary category should only happen if Casey has used it in coursework + at least one tooling/personal context, and even then frame it carefully.

6. **Re-emphasize bullet points, do not invent.**
   - You may reorder bullets within a job to put the most relevant first.
   - You may rewrite a bullet's wording to surface a relevant verb or noun (e.g., "Integrated Stripe payments" → "Built and integrated REST API payment flow with Stripe" — same fact, surfaced differently).
   - You may split one dense bullet into two if both halves are relevant to the JD.
   - You may not add a bullet that describes work Casey didn't do.
   - Strong verbs only: built, designed, shipped, owned, led, integrated, migrated, optimized, deployed, configured, automated. Avoid "responsible for," "helped with," "assisted in."

7. **Adjust the Coursework line** to surface only courses relevant to the JD (from the list in Section 2). Leave the Dean's List bold prefix.

8. **Page-fit check.** The output must be exactly one page. If it spills, tighten skills lines (combine two short categories) before cutting bullets. If it is short, do not pad — leave honest whitespace.

9. **ATS compatibility check** (Section 5).

10. **File output.** Save as `Casey_Hsu_Resume_<RoleName>_<Company>.docx`. Use the same docx-js builder pattern as the existing tailored resumes (Calibri, US Letter, 0.5"/0.75" margins, simple bullets). Validate the file before presenting.

## 5. ATS-Friendliness — Hard Constraints (2026 best practices)

Modern ATS parsers are better than they were a few years ago, but the safe-format rules still hold. Violating them risks the resume being parsed into garbage and silently rejected.

**Must:**
- Single-column layout. No multi-column / sidebar designs.
- Standard font (Calibri, Arial, Helvetica, Times New Roman, Georgia). 10–11pt body, 14–16pt name.
- Standard section headings: Summary, Technical Skills, Professional Experience, Certifications & Education. Avoid clever names like "My Story" or "Toolbox."
- Real bullet characters from a list style — never typed `*` or `-` or unicode bullets pasted in.
- Dates in a consistent format on every line ("2023 – Present", "2024", or "Mar 2024 – Apr 2026" — pick one and stick with it).
- Save as `.docx` for upload. Provide PDF only when the posting explicitly requires it.
- Fit on one page for early/mid-career. Casey is one page.

**Must not:**
- No tables for layout. (Tables for actual tabular data are OK; for column layouts they are not.)
- No graphics, photos, logos, charts, skill bars, star ratings, progress rings.
- No text in headers or footers — many parsers ignore them.
- No icons (font-awesome, emoji, or otherwise).
- No invisible white-on-white keyword stuffing. Modern ATS and recruiters' AI screeners detect this and will downrank or flag.
- No first-person pronouns ("I built", "my role"). Implicit subject only.
- No "References available upon request" line. Wastes a line; assumed.
- No Objective statement (use Summary).
- No personal info beyond city + email + portfolio + LinkedIn + GitHub. No photo, age, marital status, full address, citizenship unless legally required.
- No abbreviations without their full form on first use (`CI/CD`, `LLM`, `RAG`, `SFMC` are fine — common; `KPI`, `OOP` are fine; obscure internal jargon is not).
- No clever Unicode tricks, ligatures, or pseudo-bold using mathematical alphanumeric symbols. They break parsing.

## 6. 2026-Specific Considerations

A few things have shifted in the last 18 months and matter when tailoring.

- **AI-screening is widespread.** Many recruiters run resumes through GPT-class summarizers before a human looks. The summarizer will pull the strongest 3–5 lines. Make sure those lines (the Summary and the first bullet of the most recent role) carry the strongest match to the JD.
- **AI-generated resume slop is everywhere.** Employers are pattern-matching against generic outputs. Keep Casey's voice — concrete projects, named platforms, real numbers. If a paragraph could describe any of fifty candidates, rewrite it.
- **AI tooling is now itself a marketable skill.** Casey's Ollama / local LLM / prompt engineering work is a genuine differentiator for most 2026 dev roles, not a side note. Surface it whenever the job touches AI, ML, automation, or "modern dev practices."
- **Contract/freelance experience is taken more seriously than in previous years**, especially in Toronto. Don't apologize for the lack of FTE history — frame it as ownership and breadth.
- **The "career changer" framing has become more common and less stigmatized.** Casey's culinary leadership line is genuinely useful for senior-IC roles where stakeholder/client communication is asked for. For pure junior dev roles where it's noise, shorten it to one line — but do not delete it.
- **Years-of-experience filters are still real.** If a job lists "5+ years" as a hard floor, no resume rewriting fixes a 2.5-year history. Recommend Casey skip those rather than tailor.

## 7. Common Pitfalls — Things Agents Do Wrong

When you tailor, audit your output against this list before delivering.

1. **Promoted "Familiar" silently.** Did the rewrite move Python or Java from "Familiar" to a primary category without clear coursework + tooling justification? Revert.
2. **Invented metrics.** Did a bullet pick up a "+20%" or "led team of 6" that wasn't in the original? Remove it.
3. **Job-description echo.** Did a bullet just paraphrase the JD requirements back at the reader? That's a tell. Rewrite using Casey's actual project specifics.
4. **Stack name dropping.** Did the skills section gain a tool Casey doesn't use, because it appeared in the JD's "preferred" list? Remove it.
5. **Lost honesty signals.** Did the rewrite delete the "Familiar" category entirely to make Casey look stronger? Restore it; recruiters appreciate signal.
6. **Spilled to two pages.** Tighten skill rows or combine categories before cutting real content.
7. **Lost the AI tooling line** when the JD bonus skills mention AI. Re-add it.
8. **Replaced the original voice with LLM boilerplate.** "Spearheaded synergistic solutions" / "leveraged cutting-edge technologies." Rewrite with concrete nouns and verbs.
9. **Generic summary.** "Passionate developer who loves solving problems." This says nothing. Always make the summary specific to this role.
10. **Wrong company in filename or document.** Sanity-check before delivery.
11. **Broken URL or job ID.** If you reference a posting URL, verify it resolves to the role described — Casey caught this exact bug already.
12. **Coursework not relevant.** Listing every GBC course is noise. Pick 4–6 that match the JD.

You are not just a writer; you are a check on the application strategy. Speak up when:

- **The JD requires a hard skill Casey doesn't have** as a must-have (e.g., "5+ years SFMC + AmpScript" — no resume tailoring fixes this). Recommend skip.
- **The years gap is more than 2x** what Casey has. (5+ year roles for a 2.5-year candidate.)
- **The role is in a city Casey isn't in** and isn't remote. He is in Toronto.
- **The seniority is wrong** (Senior, Staff, Lead, Principal, Architect). These titles imply not just years but org influence Casey hasn't built.
- **The domain requires regulated experience** Casey doesn't have (clinical software, securities trading systems, medical devices). Tailoring won't get past compliance screening.
- **The title is people-management or non-IC** (Manager, Senior Manager, Director, Head of, VP — including "Engineering Manager"). Casey is an IC engineer, not a people leader.
- **The title is a non-engineering function** (Sales, Partnerships, Account Executive/Manager, Customer Success, Marketing, Product/Project/Program Manager, Recruiter, Designer, Analyst, non-technical Consultant). Only hands-on coding roles qualify.

A short, honest "this isn't a realistic fit and here's why" saves Casey time. He has explicitly asked for honesty, not optimism.
